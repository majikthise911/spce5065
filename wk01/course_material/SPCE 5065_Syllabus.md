Course Description:

Introduction to the properties and effects of the environment in which spacecraft and astronauts must operate. Intensive coverage is given to the earth-sun-lunar system. Topics include earth's environment ionosphere, atmospheric chemistry, radiation belts, magnetosphere, aurora, geomagnetic storms, celestial background, and bio-astronautic effects.

Course Introduction:

Space is a place and, like going anywhere, we must prepare. The space environment is harsh and hostile to both spacecraft and humans. To design a successful mission, we must understand how the space environment affects spacecraft performance and lifetime. These interactions can lead to malfunctions, damage, and component or subsystem losses. It is critical to understand the space environment so we can design successful missions.

Of course, not only is the space environment harsh on satellites, but it is also harmful to humans when not carefully planned for. In the final section of this course, we will examine the broad range of environmental stresses on the human element. After completing this course, it will enhance your understanding of the overall space mission design process, and you will be better prepared for your career.

### Expected Course Outcomes:

- • Demonstrate base knowledge of the effect of different aspects of the space environment including space weather hazards and associated risks
- • Describe solar and space weather phenomena, including aurora, coronal holes, coronal mass ejections (CME), solar flares, sunspots, solar cycle, geomagnetic storms, characteristics of the magnetosphere, and behavior of the interaction between different elements
- • Quantify the influence of the space environment on spacecraft and their components
- • Design spacecraft robust to space environmental hazards
- • Examine bioastronautics risks to humans due to the space environment and mitigation techniques
- • Perform detailed analysis to determine effects of space phenomena
- • Develop simulations to model space environmental hazards and effects on spacecraft


### Course Outline:

The course is divided into roughly six parts:

- • The vacuum environment
- • Neutral atmospheric particles
- • Charged particle and plasma interactions with the spacecraft
- • The radiation environment
- • Orbital debris
- • Human effects

### Dr. George’s Introduction

My name is Dr. Lynnane George, and I have been teaching in one capacity or another for 27 years. I began my teaching career as a graduate student at Georgia Tech, where I taught an undergraduate Mechanical Measurements course. As a first-generation college graduate, I initially felt uncertain in my new role, an experience that helped me develop empathy for students who may be navigating unfamiliar academic environments.

Following graduate school, I served for 20 years in the United States Air Force, working on space systems, launch vehicles, and satellite programs. I was later selected to teach in the Department of Astronautics at the United States Air Force Academy, where I discovered my passion for teaching and mentoring students. After retiring from military service, I pursued a second career in higher education and now serve as an Associate Teaching Professor in Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering. Throughout my career, I have taught students from a wide range of backgrounds and experiences. These interactions have reinforced my belief that all students can succeed when provided with meaningful opportunities to engage, practice, and grow.

Personal Teaching Philosophy My teaching philosophy centers on active learning. I also believe that learning requires practice, feedback, and reflection. Students should have multiple opportunities to develop skills before they are formally assessed. By providing guided practice and timely feedback, I strive to build both competence and confidence.

Equally important, students learn best when they feel respected, supported, and valued. Because I was a first-generation college student myself, I am particularly aware that students enter the classroom with different levels of preparation and familiarity with higher education. My goal is to create a learning environment where all students feel comfortable participating, asking questions, and contributing their perspectives.
